# Tax Considerations for Freelancers

This guide covers basic tax considerations for freelance developers. **Important: This is informational only. Consult with a qualified tax professional (CPA or enrolled agent) for tax advice specific to your situation.**

## Overview

As a freelancer, you're responsible for:
- Tracking all income
- Tracking all deductible expenses
- Paying estimated quarterly taxes
- Filing annual tax return
- Paying self-employment tax

## Business Structure and Taxes

### Sole Proprietorship

**Tax Treatment:**
- Income reported on Schedule C (Form 1040)
- Self-employment tax on net income
- Income tax on net income
- No separate business tax return

**Pros:**
- Simple
- No separate tax return
- Easy to set up

**Cons:**
- Self-employment tax (15.3%)
- Personal liability
- Higher tax rates at higher income

---

### LLC (Single Member)

**Tax Treatment:**
- Default: Treated as sole proprietorship (Schedule C)
- Can elect S-Corp taxation (if qualified)
- Can elect C-Corp taxation (rare)

**Pros:**
- Liability protection
- Tax flexibility
- Can elect S-Corp to reduce self-employment tax

**Cons:**
- More complex than sole proprietorship
- May have state fees
- S-Corp election requires payroll

---

### S-Corporation

**Tax Treatment:**
- Pass-through taxation
- Pay yourself reasonable salary (subject to payroll taxes)
- Remaining profit not subject to self-employment tax
- File Form 1120-S

**Pros:**
- Can reduce self-employment tax
- Liability protection
- Professional structure

**Cons:**
- More complex
- Requires payroll
- More paperwork
- Must pay reasonable salary

---

## Income Tracking

### What Counts as Income

- Client payments
- Retainer payments
- Project payments
- Consulting fees
- Any money received for services

### How to Track

- Use accounting software
- Keep all invoices
- Record all payments
- Track by client and project
- Organize by tax year

### Reporting

- Report all income (even if not invoiced)
- Report in year received (cash basis) or earned (accrual basis)
- Most freelancers use cash basis

---

## Deductible Expenses

### Common Deductions

**Home Office:**
- If you work from home exclusively
- Simplified method: $5/sq ft (max 300 sq ft = $1,500)
- Actual expense method: Calculate percentage of home used

**Equipment:**
- Computers, monitors, peripherals
- Software and subscriptions
- Office furniture
- Can deduct full cost (Section 179) or depreciate

**Software & Subscriptions:**
- Development tools
- Cloud services
- Business software
- Professional subscriptions

**Internet & Phone:**
- Business portion of internet
- Business phone or business portion of cell phone
- Can deduct 100% if business-only

**Professional Services:**
- Legal fees
- Accounting fees
- Consulting fees
- Professional memberships

**Marketing & Advertising:**
- Website hosting
- Domain names
- Advertising
- Business cards
- Portfolio website

**Travel:**
- Business travel (hotels, flights, meals)
- Local business travel (mileage or actual expenses)
- Standard mileage rate: $0.655/mile (2024)

**Meals:**
- Business meals (50% deductible)
- Client entertainment (generally not deductible)
- Keep receipts and note business purpose

**Education & Training:**
- Courses related to business
- Conferences
- Books and materials
- Professional development

**Insurance:**
- Business insurance
- Health insurance (if self-employed)
- Professional liability insurance

**Other:**
- Bank fees
- Payment processing fees
- Office supplies
- Postage and shipping

### What's NOT Deductible

- Personal expenses
- Commuting to regular workplace
- Personal meals (unless business-related)
- Hobby expenses (if not profit-making)
- Fines and penalties

## Self-Employment Tax

### What It Is

- Social Security tax (12.4%)
- Medicare tax (2.9%)
- Total: 15.3% on net self-employment income
- Applies to first $160,200 of income (2024, Social Security portion)
- Medicare applies to all income

### How It Works

- Calculated on Schedule SE
- Based on net income (income minus expenses)
- Half is deductible on income tax return
- Paid through estimated taxes

### Reducing Self-Employment Tax

- Maximize business deductions
- Consider S-Corp election (if income is high enough)
- Consult tax professional

## Estimated Quarterly Taxes

### When Required

- If you expect to owe $1,000+ in taxes
- If withholding won't cover tax liability
- Most freelancers need to pay quarterly

### Due Dates

- **Q1 (Jan-Mar):** April 15
- **Q2 (Apr-Jun):** June 15
- **Q3 (Jul-Sep):** September 15
- **Q4 (Oct-Dec):** January 15 (next year)

### How to Calculate

**Safe Harbor Method:**
- Pay 100% of previous year's tax (110% if income > $150,000)
- Split into 4 equal payments

**Annualized Method:**
- Estimate current year's income
- Calculate tax based on year-to-date income
- Pay accordingly

**Recommendation:** Use safe harbor method for simplicity

### How to Pay

- **Online:** IRS.gov (Direct Pay or EFTPS)
- **Mail:** Form 1040-ES with payment voucher
- **Phone:** 1-800-829-1040

### Penalties

- Underpayment penalty if you don't pay enough
- Interest on underpayment
- Avoid by paying safe harbor amount

## Annual Tax Return

### Forms Needed

**For Sole Proprietor/LLC:**
- Form 1040 (Individual Tax Return)
- Schedule C (Profit or Loss from Business)
- Schedule SE (Self-Employment Tax)
- Form 1040-ES (if paying estimated taxes)

**Additional Forms (if applicable):**
- Schedule EIC (if eligible for earned income credit)
- Form 8829 (Home Office Deduction - if using actual expense method)
- Form 4562 (Depreciation - if depreciating assets)

### Filing Deadline

- **Individual Return:** April 15 (or next business day)
- **Extension:** Can file for extension (Form 4868) to October 15
- **Note:** Extension extends filing deadline, not payment deadline

### Tax Preparation

**Options:**
1. **DIY:** TurboTax, H&R Block, Free File
2. **Tax Professional:** CPA or enrolled agent (recommended for first year)
3. **Tax Software:** Good for simple situations

**Recommendation:** Use tax professional for first year, then consider DIY if situation is simple

## Record Keeping

### What to Keep

- All income records (invoices, payments)
- All expense receipts
- Bank statements
- Credit card statements
- Tax documents
- Previous tax returns

### How Long to Keep

- **Tax Returns:** Forever (recommended)
- **Supporting Documents:** 3-7 years (IRS can audit up to 3 years, 6 years for substantial understatement)
- **Business Records:** 7 years (recommended)

### Organization

- Organize by year
- Digital storage (scanned receipts)
- Backup regularly
- Easy to access

## State Taxes

### Income Tax

- Most states have income tax
- Report on state tax return
- May need to pay estimated state taxes

### Sales Tax

- If selling products (not services typically)
- Check state requirements
- May need sales tax permit
- Collect and remit sales tax

### Business Tax

- Some states/cities have business tax
- Check local requirements
- May need business license

---

## Tax Planning Strategies

### 1. Maximize Deductions

- Track all business expenses
- Don't miss deductible items
- Keep receipts organized

### 2. Retirement Contributions

- SEP-IRA: Up to 25% of net income (max $69,000 for 2024)
- Solo 401(k): Up to $69,000 for 2024
- Traditional IRA: Up to $7,000 for 2024
- Reduces taxable income

### 3. Health Savings Account (HSA)

- If you have high-deductible health plan
- Up to $4,150 (individual) or $8,300 (family) for 2024
- Tax-deductible contributions
- Tax-free withdrawals for medical expenses

### 4. Timing Income and Expenses

- Can time when you receive income (within reason)
- Can time when you pay expenses
- Consult tax professional for strategies

### 5. Quarterly Tax Planning

- Review income quarterly
- Adjust estimated payments if needed
- Plan for tax liability

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Not Paying Estimated Taxes:** Can result in penalties
2. **Mixing Personal and Business:** Keep separate accounts
3. **Not Keeping Receipts:** Need proof for deductions
4. **Not Tracking All Income:** Must report all income
5. **Missing Deductions:** Track all business expenses
6. **Not Consulting Professional:** Get help for complex situations

---

## When to Consult a Tax Professional

**Definitely Consult:**
- First year in business
- Complex tax situations
- High income ($100,000+)
- Multiple income sources
- S-Corp election
- International clients
- Tax issues or audits

**May Not Need:**
- Simple situation
- Low income
- Single source of income
- Using tax software successfully

**Cost:** $200-500 typically for tax preparation, but worth it for peace of mind
